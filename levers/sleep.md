# Lever: Sleep

> Lever complete for ranking. The defining feature of this lever: large observational HRs but **thin causality** — most of the mortality signal is reverse causation (illness disrupts sleep). Two genuine causal bright spots (MR insomnia→CAD; CBT-I→depression). Reversibility is a real evidence gap (parked).

## Scope
Duration, consolidation, circadian alignment, recovery. The downstream-of-everything lever — and the one most entangled with reverse causation (illness disrupts sleep as much as sleep disrupts health).

- In: sleep duration (the U-shaped mortality curve); sleep quality / consolidation / efficiency / fragmentation; circadian alignment & timing (shift work, social jetlag, sleep regularity, chronotype); major sleep disorders as mortality/CVD drivers (insomnia, obstructive sleep apnea); napping; behavioral sleep hygiene (light exposure, screens, timing); and INTERVENTION evidence — does improving sleep (CBT-I, treating OSA/CPAP, sleep extension) change hard endpoints?
- Out (and where it lives instead): exercise-as-recovery / training adaptation → `exercise`; caffeine & alcohol *as substances* → `substances` (their sleep-disruption mechanism is noted here, but the mortality claim stays there); sleep's metabolic/glucose markers (insulin sensitivity, weight) → `nutrition-metabolic` (the CVD/mortality endpoint stays here); depression/anxiety as drivers of insomnia → `stress-social` (bidirectional — flagged, not owned here); dementia mechanism deep-dive (glymphatic/amyloid) → ticked in `neurological`, but the sleep→dementia risk claim stays here; melatonin/pharmacologic sleep aids → basic sleep-aid evidence noted here, deep supplement analysis deferred.

## Dose / threshold
**The 80/20 is unusual for this lever: the dose target is real but the *causal payoff* of hitting it is smaller than the scary HRs imply.**

- **Duration:** aim for **~7–7.5h** — the consistent nadir for mortality, CVD, cerebrovascular disease, and stroke. But two honest caveats: (a) the *long-sleep* danger zone (≥9h) is mostly sick people sleeping more, not sleep harming you — don't fear-set an alarm to cap sleep; (b) the two biggest meta-analyses don't even agree that *short* sleep (<7h) raises mortality. Treat 7–7.5h as a sensible target, not a knife-edge.
- **Regularity beats duration heroics:** the best-measured signal is *irregular* timing (objective SRI → CVD-mortality HR 1.88). Consistent sleep/wake times are likely higher-yield than chasing an exact duration. (Still observational.)
- **Insomnia / quality:** the actionable move is **CBT-I**, not sleeping pills — it's first-line, durable ~1 year, and (the real prize) roughly halves incident depression. This is the one place "fixing sleep" has hard-endpoint causal evidence.
- **OSA:** if you have moderate–severe OSA, treat it for **symptoms** (sleepiness, BP, quality of life) — but do **not** expect CPAP to prevent heart attacks; the RCTs are null. Adherence <4h/night is the norm and part of why.
- **Where the lever is NOT:** chasing specific sleep *stages*, glymphatic "brain detox," or wearable scores has no hard-outcome evidence. Don't optimize the surrogate.

## Actions (behavioral, checkable)
- [ ] Target a **regular ~7–7.5h** sleep opportunity, with consistent sleep/wake times (regularity may matter more than the exact number).
- [ ] Don't catastrophize occasional short nights or naturally longer sleep — the long-sleep "risk" is mostly reverse causation, and acute debt is largely recoverable.
- [ ] If you have chronic insomnia, do **CBT-I** (in person or a validated digital program) — first-line, durable, and the one sleep intervention with a hard causal payoff (≈49% lower incident depression). Avoid hypnotics as the default.
- [ ] If you snore heavily / have witnessed apneas / daytime sleepiness, get screened for **OSA** and treat it — for *symptoms and BP*, not as a heart-attack-prevention strategy. If you use CPAP, aim for >4h/night or it does little.
- [ ] Protect sleep as a *depression and daytime-function* lever (its best-evidenced benefits), not as a longevity silver bullet — the mortality HRs overstate the causal stakes.

## Caveats / population modifiers
- **Causality is the whole story.** Of ~62 confirmed claims, the genuinely causal ones are narrow: MR (insomnia→CAD/HTN, #6) and two RCT threads (CBT-I→depression #12; CPAP→*no* MACE #8). Everything else is observational with **reverse causation as a live, author-acknowledged confounder** — illness, depression, and low SES cause poor/long sleep, inflating every HR. The large numbers are real *associations* but weak *levers*.
- **The OSA paradox is the teaching case:** strong observational risk (mortality HR up to 2.45) + null CPAP RCTs (MACE OR 0.94). Either the risk is confounded, or CPAP adherence is too poor to realize it — probably both. Don't let the observational number set the expectation for treatment benefit.
- **Dementia link is mostly prodromal:** sleep changes appear years before diagnosis (AD has a ~20-yr prodrome), MR is null, and the glymphatic story is surrogate-only. Sleep is more *early-warning sign* than *cause* of dementia.
- **CBT-I evidence is strong for insomnia/depression, thinner downstream:** the depression result is one large single-site RCT in *older* adults (does it generalize to mid-life?); the inflammation/CRP benefit is one single-lab RCT and may not be sleep-specific (Tai Chi did better). No RCT shows CBT-I or sleep extension lowering CV events or mortality.
- **Sleep extension** trials are small (n=80), short (2 wk), and surrogate; the weight loss was mostly *fat-free* mass (not clearly good), and the insulin-sensitivity claim was refuted. Promising for appetite, not established for cardiometabolic health.
- **Reversibility is genuinely unresolved here** (#15) — physiologically acute debt is largely FAST-recoverable and chronic risk likely SLOW-normalizing, but no verified claim pins it. Don't assert "sleep banking" or "weekend catch-up erases the risk."
- **Most cohort data is self-reported** (recall bias); the SRI/UK Biobank study (objective accelerometry) is the notable, stronger exception. Several duration meta-analyses share overlapping author groups (reduced independence).

## Open questions
*(also copied to `synthesis/open-questions.md`)*
- **Reversibility tiers (FAST/SLOW/IRREVERSIBLE)** — acute-debt full recovery vs persistent deficits, "sleep banking" (Rupp/Wesensten), weekend catch-up offsetting weekday risk, and chronic-restriction normalization. None survived verification — the one part of the question still genuinely open. A dedicated pass could close it, but it's low decision-relevance (the actions don't change).
- **Does CBT-I prevent depression in non-elderly adults?** The hard-endpoint RCT is older-adults, single-site. Generalization + multi-site replication unknown.
- **Any RCT evidence that improving sleep lowers hard CV events / BP / mortality?** The confirmed set has none (CBT-I and sleep extension only touch surrogates/depression). True gap vs out-of-scope-search — unclear.
- **Is the CBT-I anti-inflammatory effect sleep-specific?** Tai Chi Chih produced more durable cellular effects than CBT-I in the same trial — suggests the inflammation benefit may be a general behavioral-intervention effect, not sleep per se.
- **Short-sleep causality** — the two biggest meta-analyses disagree on whether <7h raises mortality at all; MR-insomnia suggests *some* causal cardiometabolic effect, but the duration-specific causal magnitude is unresolved.
