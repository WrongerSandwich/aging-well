## Scope
Tobacco, alcohol, other recreational/psychoactive substances. The single largest modifiable mortality cluster.

- In: cigarette smoking (incl. low-intensity), smokeless tobacco, alcohol, nicotine vaping (attempted), cannabis (attempted).
- Out (and where it lives instead): caffeine → `nutrition-metabolic`; substance use as a stress-coping mechanism / social drinking dynamics → `stress-social`; medication/polypharmacy → `medical-screening`; oral cancer screening & dental effects of tobacco → ticked in `oral-sensory` but the mortality claim stays here.

## Dose / threshold
**The 80/20:** for both tobacco and alcohol the curve has **no safe floor for the net outcome** — the gain is almost entirely in *not starting* / *full cessation*, not in moderation.

- **Tobacco:** dose-response is steep from the very first cigarette (<1 CPD already HR 1.64). There is no "light smoking is basically fine" tier. The single highest-value move is total cessation; intensity reduction helps far less than quitting.
- **Cessation timing:** benefit is steeply front-loaded by age — quit-before-40 recovers ~90%, and benefit is *detectable within 3 years*. Returns diminish with age but stay positive into the 70s.
- **Alcohol:** harm-minimizing intake across all outcomes = **0 drinks/week.** The only protective window (IHD) sits near ~1 drink/day and is wiped out by monotonically rising cancer risk. Treat "moderate drinking is healthy" as overturned at the net level. Practical floor for *meaningfully lower* (not zero) harm is roughly ≤1 drink/day, but lower is always better and the gradient never reverses for cancer.
- **Vaping:** not a dose question so much as a *comparator* question. vs **smoking**, it's clearly less harmful (esp. respiratory/COPD) — but the famous "95% safer" number is a 2015 expert estimate, not measured, and the real CVD gap is more like 30–40%, not 95%. vs **never-use**, it is NOT harm-free (composite-CVD AOR ~1.31, respiratory RR ~1.90). Crucially, the scary MI signals are concentrated in *former smokers* who vape; exclusive never-smoker vapers show little MI elevation. And no long-term mortality cohort exists yet, so all of this is T2-capped. Net: a harm-*reduction* tool for smokers, not a health-neutral habit for non-smokers.

## Actions (behavioral, checkable)
- [ ] Don't smoke. If you do: quit — this is the single highest-leverage action in the entire project. (Benefit detectable in 3 yrs; ~90% recovery if done before 40.)
- [ ] Avoid smokeless tobacco as a "safe" substitute — smaller penalty than cigarettes but still HR ~1.36.
- [ ] Treat alcohol as a net-negative with no health-justified floor. Anchor on ≤1 drink/day as a harm-*reduction* ceiling, understanding 0 is optimal for total health.
- [ ] Don't take up vaping as a non-smoker — it is not harm-free and its long-term risks are literally unmeasured.
- [ ] If you're a smoker who can't quit outright, a *complete* switch to vaping (not dual use) is a defensible harm-reduction step — but it's a cessation tactic, not a safe habit. Dual use buys nothing.
- [ ] (Deferred) Decide on cannabis once researched — still unscored; do not assume "safe."

## Caveats / population modifiers
- **Smoking HRs** are adjusted for age, education, adiposity, and alcohol — these are the substance's own effect, not lifestyle confounds.
- **Finding #7 (older-age cessation) is a life-table SIMULATION** (CPS-II relative risks + 2018 prevalence), not directly observed cohort deltas. Tagged T1-input but confidence M.
- **Smokeless tobacco heart-disease** evidence is genuinely unsettled; the "no excess" result leans on an Altria-affiliated study (rows #8/#11). All-cause penalty (#8) is solid; the cardiac-specific magnitude is not.
- **Alcohol "zero is optimal"** is the robust *net-across-outcomes* conclusion. The 2022 GBD update added age-specific thresholds suggesting a small possible net benefit for *some older adults* — so this is not "one drink will kill you," it's "there is no population-level health reason to start or maintain drinking." The IHD-specific protection (#10) is real but is the least causally-secure piece (MR debate ongoing).
- **Reverse causation** ("sick-quitter" effect) can inflate short-term mortality in some late-quitter subgroups; it does not overturn the 3-year benefit signal or the core cessation conclusions.
- All cessation "years averted/restored" figures are observational ("potentially averted"), not RCT-derived.
- **Vaping evidence is immature and largely cross-sectional.** Hard-endpoint claims (#11–#15) are T2-capped because no long-term cohort exists and most data can't establish causality/temporality (reverse causation: people may switch to vaping *after* a cardiac event). The MI signal is confounded by smoking history. Do not let vaping's T2 rows outrank tobacco/alcohol T1 conclusions.
- **EVALI ≠ regulated nicotine vaping.** The 2019 lung-injury outbreak was traced to illicit THC cartridges with vitamin-E-acetate, not regulated nicotine e-liquid — relevant context but a separate phenomenon (not separately verified here; sources noted in `_meta/sources.md`).
