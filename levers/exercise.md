## Scope
Cardiorespiratory fitness, resistance/strength, mobility/balance. The second-largest modifiable cluster after substances — and unlike substances, almost entirely a "do more" rather than "stop" lever.

- In: aerobic/cardiorespiratory training and measured fitness (VO2max / METs); resistance/strength training (incl. grip strength as a marker); mobility / balance / flexibility training; dose-response and minimum effective dose; detraining/retraining (reversibility); falls & fracture prevention via the exercise route.
- Out (and where it lives instead): post-exercise recovery & sleep → `sleep`; protein and dietary intake to support training → `nutrition-metabolic`; sedentary-behavior metabolic effects (sitting, glucose) → noted here only where they share a mortality endpoint, otherwise `nutrition-metabolic`; orthopedic injury management / DEXA & screening decisions → `medical-screening` (the fracture *risk reduction* claim stays here); cognitive/mood benefits of exercise → ticked in `neurological`/`stress-social` but the mortality/healthspan claims stay here.

## Dose / threshold
**The 80/20:** unlike substances (where the win is total cessation), exercise's win is **getting off zero**. The dose-response is steepest at the bottom; the first ~150 min/week of moderate activity buys most of the *mortality* benefit, and returns flatten well before "elite" volumes. The falls/fracture/bone benefits, by contrast, are **modality-specific** — you have to do the *right kind*, not just more.

- **Aerobic — minimum effective dose:** ~150 min/wk moderate (or ~75 min vigorous) buys ≈19–21% lower all-cause mortality vs sedentary. Returns flatten by ~300 min/wk moderate / ~180 min/wk vigorous; the high-volume "reversal" (#17) is weak. Pattern is irrelevant — weekend-warrior = spread-out (#4).
- **Intensity:** measured **CRF** is the strongest predictor (each +1 MET ≈ −11–17% mortality). A *mix* of intensities (~30–60% vigorous) edges out all-out vigorous (#3).
- **Resistance — small effective dose for mortality:** ~60–119 min/wk (≈2 sessions) captures the independent benefit; **plateaus** above ~120 min/wk (#5). For *bone*, though, intensity is the lever, not volume: only **HiRIT** (heavy, ≥80% 1RM, or high-impact) builds lumbar-spine BMD; moderate lifting maintains but doesn't build (#13).
- **Balance — the falls dose:** supervised, **>3-month, ≥2×/week balance/functional training** (Otago-style, or balance+resistance) is what moves falls and fractures. Walking, stretching, or lifting *alone* do **not** prevent falls (#10). The benefit is not banked — it fades when you stop (#14, and entirely so in frail aged-care populations).
- **The best single prescription:** aerobic (for mortality) + heavy resistance/impact (for bone & strength) + a balance/functional component (for falls). The three modalities buy *different* endpoints — they're not interchangeable.

## Actions (behavioral, checkable)
- [ ] **Get off zero first.** If sedentary, reach ~150 min/week of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walk, zone-2). Buys ~1/5 of all-cause mortality risk — most of exercise's longevity benefit.
- [ ] **Build cardiorespiratory fitness as the primary longevity target.** Push some volume to higher intensity (a ~30–60% vigorous mix beats all-easy or all-hard); aim to climb MET categories over time.
- [ ] **Resistance train ~2×/week.** For *mortality* ~60–120 min/wk is enough (plateaus after). For *bone*, make some of it **heavy/high-intensity (HiRIT)** — moderate loads maintain bone but don't build it.
- [ ] **Add a dedicated balance/functional component** (single-leg work, Otago-style, or tai chi within a balance program). This is the *causal, RCT-proven* lever for falls (RaR ~0.76) and fall-related fractures (~−25–33%) — and walking/stretching/lifting alone won't deliver it.
- [ ] **Treat it as maintenance, not a deposit.** Fitness, strength, and bone gains reverse with detraining over months — the benefit requires *continuing*, which is why this is a lifelong habit, not a project.
- [ ] **Don't over-optimize scheduling.** Weekend-warrior is fine for the aerobic/mortality piece — total weekly volume is what matters.

## Caveats / population modifiers
- **Two different evidence tiers, don't blend them.** The *mortality/longevity* numbers (#1–#8) are OBSERVATIONAL — reverse causation (subclinically ill people are less fit/weaker) and healthy-adherent bias (exercisers smoke less, eat better, higher SES) inflate them; read as upper bounds. The *falls/fracture/BMD* numbers (#9–#14) are RCT INTERVENTION evidence — genuinely causal, the strongest tier in this project — but fracture is a secondary/underpowered endpoint (#12) and BMD is a surrogate for fracture.
- **Surrogate vs hard endpoint:** grip strength (#7) and balance tests (#8) *predict* mortality but aren't proof that training them lowers mortality. The case that exercise extends life rests on the cohort data (#1–#6); the case that it prevents *falls/fractures* rests on the RCTs (#9–#11). Keep those two claims separate — exercise's proven *causal* wins are falls/fracture/bone/strength, while its longevity effect, though large, is associational.
- **Modality specificity is real:** "exercise" is not one thing. Aerobic buys mortality; heavy resistance buys bone & strength; balance buys falls. Walking is great for #1–#4 and useless for falls (#10). Don't let a single word hide three different prescriptions.
- **Reversibility is SLOW, not irreversible** — the key contrast with substances. Fitness, muscle, and bone gains are lost with detraining (BMD toward baseline in ~12 mo, #14) and regained with retraining. Benefits require maintenance; a fit past doesn't bank permanent protection.
- **Population fit:** the falls/fracture and balance-predictor evidence comes from older adults (mean ages ~70s) — most relevant as you age. In *frail/residential* populations the falls benefit holds during training but **disappears once training stops** (high-certainty), so continuity matters even more there. For a healthy mid-life adult, the aerobic/CRF and resistance claims carry the weight; balance/bone work is preventive investment for later.
- **The combined-training edge over high-aerobic-alone is small for *mortality*** (#6) — don't oversell "you must lift to live longer." Lift for strength, bone, falls, and function; the marginal mortality gain over being aerobically fit is modest.

## Open questions
*(also copied to `synthesis/open-questions.md`)*
- **Sarcopenia / muscle-mass & strength intervention magnitudes** — the targeted run nailed falls/fracture/BMD but the resistance-training effect sizes on *muscle mass, strength, gait speed, chair-stand* in sarcopenic/older adults did **not** survive verification as clean numbers. Direction is certain (RT builds muscle/strength); the pooled magnitudes are still un-pinned. Lower priority — doesn't change the action ("lift heavy, ~2×/wk").
- **Muscle/strength detraining-retraining kinetics** — only *bone* detraining was quantified (~12 mo to baseline). How fast strength/muscle are lost and regained in older adults is still unquantified here. Physiologically SLOW; specifics open.
- **Resistance dose optimum** — plateau (~90–119 min/wk) vs J-curve (peak 30–60 min/wk) disagreement (#16). Both point to "~2×/wk is enough," so low decision-relevance.
- **Anti-fracture mechanism split** — how much of exercise's fracture reduction is via *falls* (mechanical) vs *BMD/bone-quality*? No adequately-powered primary-endpoint trial separates them (#12). Academic, not action-relevant.
