## Scope
Cardiorespiratory fitness, resistance/strength, mobility/balance. The second-largest modifiable cluster after substances — and unlike substances, almost entirely a "do more" rather than "stop" lever.

- In: aerobic/cardiorespiratory training and measured fitness (VO2max / METs); resistance/strength training (incl. grip strength as a marker); mobility / balance / flexibility training; dose-response and minimum effective dose; detraining/retraining (reversibility); falls & fracture prevention via the exercise route.
- Out (and where it lives instead): post-exercise recovery & sleep → `sleep`; protein and dietary intake to support training → `nutrition-metabolic`; sedentary-behavior metabolic effects (sitting, glucose) → noted here only where they share a mortality endpoint, otherwise `nutrition-metabolic`; orthopedic injury management / DEXA & screening decisions → `medical-screening` (the fracture *risk reduction* claim stays here); cognitive/mood benefits of exercise → ticked in `neurological`/`stress-social` but the mortality/healthspan claims stay here.

## Dose / threshold
**The 80/20:** unlike substances (where the win is total cessation), exercise's win is **getting off zero**. The dose-response is steepest at the very bottom; the first ~150 min/week of moderate activity buys most of the mortality benefit, and returns flatten well before "elite" volumes.

- **Aerobic — minimum effective dose:** ~150 min/wk moderate (or ~75 min vigorous) buys ≈19–21% lower all-cause mortality vs sedentary. Returns continue but diminish to ~300 min/wk moderate / ~180 min/wk vigorous, then flatten. **No U-shape or harm** at ordinary doses; the high-volume "reversal" (#11) is weak. Pattern is irrelevant — **weekend-warrior = spread-out** once weekly volume is met (#4).
- **Intensity:** measured **CRF** (VO2max/METs) is the strongest and most consistent predictor — stronger than self-reported activity (which is noisy and *attenuates* true effects). Each +1 MET ≈ −11–17% all-cause mortality. A *mix* of intensities (~30–60% vigorous) edges out all-out vigorous (#3).
- **Resistance — minimum effective dose is small:** ~60–119 min/wk (i.e. **2 short sessions**) captures the independent benefit (≈10–13% all-cause). Benefit **plateaus** above ~120 min/wk — more lifting doesn't buy more longevity (though the exact optimum is contested, #10). This is roughly "2×/week," not "every day."
- **The best single prescription:** **aerobic + resistance combined** (#6) — but be honest that the longevity increment over just hitting aerobic guidelines is small. The combo's extra value is in healthspan/function (strength, balance, independence), which the mortality HR understates.
- **Mobility/balance:** there is no clean "dose" — the T1 evidence is that *being* able to balance (10-s stance) and move (SPPB/gait) predicts survival; the evidence that *training* them reduces mortality (vs. reducing falls) wasn't established this run (see open questions).

## Actions (behavioral, checkable)
- [ ] **Get off zero first.** If sedentary, the single highest-value move is reaching ~150 min/week of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walk, zone-2). This buys ~1/5 of all-cause mortality risk — most of exercise's benefit.
- [ ] **Build cardiorespiratory fitness as the primary target** (it's the strongest predictor). Push some of that volume to higher intensity — a ~30–60% vigorous mix beats all-easy or all-hard. Aim to move *up MET categories* over time.
- [ ] **Resistance train ~2×/week (≈60–120 min/week total).** Independent longevity benefit; plateaus after, so don't over-invest volume here for *mortality* (do more if you want strength/physique). Covers muscle mass, bone, and grip/balance capacity that predict survival.
- [ ] **Don't agonize over scheduling.** Weekend-warrior is fine — total weekly volume is what matters, not spreading it evenly.
- [ ] **Combine aerobic + resistance** for the best mortality number and the healthspan upside (function, falls, independence) that the mortality HR understates.
- [ ] **Treat balance/mobility as healthspan insurance, not a longevity lever per se.** Keep the capacity to hold a 10-second one-leg stance and rise from the floor — failing these flags risk. (Whether training them cuts *mortality* is unproven; that they prevent *falls* is the stronger rationale — pending follow-up.)

## Caveats / population modifiers
- **All observational — no RCT mortality endpoints.** Every HR here is from cohorts/meta-analyses. Two confounds inflate all effect sizes and can't be fully removed: **(1) reverse causation** — subclinically ill people are less fit, weaker, and fail balance tests, so part of the gradient is pre-existing illness, not exercise; **(2) healthy-adherent bias** — exercisers smoke less, eat better, have higher SES. Better studies mitigate (e.g. excluding the first 2–5 yrs of follow-up) but don't eliminate these. Read the magnitudes as *upper bounds* on the causal effect.
- **Surrogate vs hard endpoint:** grip strength (#7) and balance/SPPB (#8) are mortality *predictors/biomarkers* — robustly linked to death but **not** proof that training grip or balance lowers mortality. The case that resistance/balance training extends life rests on the RT *cohort* data (#5), not on these markers. Don't let a surrogate masquerade as an intervention outcome.
- **Measured vs self-reported:** directly measured CRF (#1) is the most reliable signal; the VPA dose-response (#3) and pattern data (#4) rest on *self-reported* activity, which adds error and generally *attenuates* true effects (so those may be underestimates).
- **Reversibility is SLOW, not irreversible** — and this is the key contrast with substances. Fitness and muscle are *lost* with detraining and *regained* with retraining over weeks–months. (Exact detraining/retraining kinetics were not quantified this run — open question.) The corollary: benefits require *maintenance*; a fit past doesn't bank permanent protection the way quitting smoking eventually does.
- **Population fit:** the balance/functional-test HRs (#8) come from older cohorts (mean age ~76 for SPPB; ages 51–75 for the stance test) — they're most informative for older adults, less so for a healthy mid-life adult where the aerobic/CRF and resistance claims carry the weight.
- **The combined-training edge over high-aerobic-alone is real but small** (#6) — don't oversell "you must lift to live longer." Lift for strength, function, and falls; the marginal *mortality* gain over just being aerobically fit is modest.

## Open questions
*(also copied to `synthesis/open-questions.md`)*
- **Reversibility / detraining kinetics** — how fast is CRF and strength lost with detraining and regained with retraining, and is any portion irreversible? Explicitly requested; **no surviving claim addressed it.** Physiologically SLOW-reversible, but magnitudes/timelines are unquantified here.
- **Resistance / balance INTERVENTION outcomes beyond mortality** — quantified effects of resistance & balance training on **sarcopenia / muscle mass, bone mineral density, fracture risk, and fall risk** (RR/HR) in older adults. This run found strong *predictor* evidence (grip, SPPB, stance) but did **not** verify that *training* moves these hard endpoints. This is the biggest scope gap — falls/fracture prevention is in-scope and has known RCT evidence (Otago, tai-chi, Cochrane) that a targeted follow-up could pull in.
- **Measured vs self-reported, head-to-head** — is directly measured CRF a *quantifiably* stronger mortality predictor than self-reported activity in the same population? Data suggest yes; no surviving claim makes the direct comparison.
- **Resistance dose optimum** — why do the two strongest cohorts disagree (plateau at 90–119 min/wk vs J-curve peaking 30–60 min/wk), and is there genuine loss of benefit at high volume? (#10)
- Gait-speed and sit-to-stand mortality HRs were requested as additional functional predictors but were not among the surviving verified claims.
